## Cautions and Challenges from Science

### Benefits of Science in Human Development
- Science helps us understand human development using **facts and evidence**.
- It has improved life in many ways:
  - Fewer infectious diseases in children
  - Less illiteracy in adolescents
  - Reduced racism and sexism over time
  - Lower rates of early death (accidents, war, disease)
- Science has also revealed hidden dangers, such as:
  - Lead and asbestos exposure
  - Vaping risks
  - Some pregnancy, childhood, and technology-related risks (e.g., excessive weight gain, video games, hospitalization risks)

## 1. Correlation vs. Causation (Major Scientific Problem)

### Correlation
- A **correlation** means two variables are related (they change together in some way).
- Types:
  - **Positive correlation**: both increase or decrease together
  - **Negative correlation**: one increases while the other decreases
  - **Zero correlation**: no relationship

### Key Idea
- **Correlation is not causation**
  - Just because two things are related does NOT mean one causes the other.

### Why correlations happen:
1. A → B (one causes the other)
2. B → A (reverse cause)
3. C → A and B (third variable causes both)

### Important facts
- Correlations range from **-1.0 to +1.0**
- Real-world correlations are usually small:
  - ±0.3 = meaningful
  - ±0.8 = very strong/unusual

### Example idea
- Ice cream sales and murder rates may rise together, but **heat (third variable)** explains both.

## 2. Quantity vs. Quality in Research

### Quantitative Research (numbers)
- Uses **data, statistics, measurements**
- Strengths:
  - Easy to compare and replicate
  - Less biased
  - Works across cultures
- Limitation:
  - Misses personal experience and meaning

### Qualitative Research (meaning)
- Uses **interviews, descriptions, stories**
- Focuses on:
  - How people experience life
  - How they interpret their world
- Strength:
  - Rich detail and cultural understanding
- Limitation:
  - Harder to measure and compare

### Key Idea
- Quantitative = “how much / how many”
- Qualitative = “why / how people feel and think”

## Opposing Perspectives (Testing & Measurement)

### Education and Health Debate
- **Pro test scores:**
  - Objective, measurable
  - Helps compare schools and performance
- **Against test scores:**
  - Ignores creativity, values, and emotions
  - Can narrow education to “just numbers”

### Health example:
- Numbers (heart rate, oxygen) may show someone is “alive”
- But quality of life may tell a different story

### Best approach
- Combine both:
  - Quantitative = measurable facts
  - Qualitative = real human experience
- Together they give a fuller understanding of development

## 3. Ethics in Research (Most Important Rule)

- Scientists must follow **ethical standards**.
- Ethics = moral rules for research.

### How ethics are enforced:
- Professional organizations have **codes of ethics**
- Schools and hospitals use an **Institutional Review Board (IRB)**

### Purpose of ethics:
- Protect participants from harm
- Ensure fair and honest research
- Follow government and professional guidelines

---

## Big Idea Summary
- Science improves life but must be used carefully.
- Major challenges:
  - Misreading correlation as causation
  - Over-relying on numbers or ignoring them
  - Ensuring ethical research practices
- Best understanding comes from combining methods and perspectives.
